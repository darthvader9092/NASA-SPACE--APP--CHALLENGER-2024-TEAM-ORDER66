Exoplanets, or extrasolar planets, are planets that orbit stars outside our solar system.

They vary widely in size, composition, and distance from their host stars.

Detection methods include the transit method, where a planet passes in front of its star, causing a temporary dimming, and the radial velocity method, which measures the star's wobble due to gravitational interactions with the planet.

Thousands of exoplanets have been discovered, some located in the habitable zone where conditions might allow for liquid water, raising the possibility of life beyond Earth.


SPACE NEWS




Exoplanets are primarily detected using several key methods, each with distinct advantages.

The "Transit method" is one of the most successful techniques, where astronomers monitor the brightness of stars over time.

When a planet passes in front of its host star, it causes a temporary dip in the star’s brightness, allowing researchers to infer the planet’s size and orbit.

The "radial velocity method" complements this by measuring the star's wobble caused by the gravitational pull of an orbiting planet.

This method detects shifts in the star’s spectrum, revealing the planet's mass and orbit.

Other techniques include direct imaging, where telescopes capture images of exoplanets by blocking out the star's light, and **gravitational microlensing, which uses the gravitational field of a star to magnify the light from a more distant background star, revealing the presence of planets.

Each method has contributed to a diverse catalog of exoplanets, enhancing our understanding of their properties and distribution across the galaxy.

Exoplanets can be classified into several types: "Gas giants" like Jupiter and Saturn are large and composed mainly of hydrogen and helium.

"Ice giants, such as Uranus and Neptune, contain significant amounts of water and ammonia ices."

"Terrestrial planets" are rocky, with solid surfaces like Earth and Mars.

"Super-Earths" are larger than Earth but smaller than gas giants, often found in habitable zones.

"Mini-Neptunes" are smaller gas giants with thick atmospheres.

"Hot Jupiters" orbit close to their stars, resulting in high temperatures, while "Cold gas giants" are found farther away and are cooler.

"Pulsar planets" orbit pulsars, and "Rogue planets" drift through space without orbiting a star. Each type offers insights into planetary formation and the potential for extraterrestrial life.
